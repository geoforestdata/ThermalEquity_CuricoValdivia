How this works

How the red hexagons are built

Each hexagon combines two satellite measurements — vegetation cover (NDVI) and land surface temperature — from Landsat 8/9. The two are standardized and combined into a single Cooling Index, always measured against that city's own average, never against an outside benchmark. Red hexagons are the 25% of a city with the least relative vegetation and the most relative heat, compared to their own neighbors.

Why non-census population data is good enough here

Population comes from WorldPop, a satellite-based model that updates annually instead of waiting a decade for the next census. It's an estimate, not a door-to-door count — but at this scale, that's the right level of precision. What matters isn't whether a hexagon holds 380 or 410 people; it's whether that zone concentrates a lot of people or very few, relative to the rest of the city. Good enough to decide with, even if not good enough to audit with.

Two lenses on the same grid

Heat islands: where is the environmental problem itself — which zones physically trap heat and lack vegetation? Exposure concentration: cross the same grid with population, and the question shifts from "where is it hot" to "how many people live with it." Two equally hot hexagons can hold 50 people or 900 — the intervention priority isn't the same.

Value for high-level decisions

A city doesn't need to wait for the next census or commission a dedicated study to know, with defensible precision, where to prioritize tree planting or shade infrastructure — not "it's hot here" in the abstract, but "this zone concentrates both the heat and the people exposed to it, which is why it goes first."

Cooling Index = z(NDVI) − z(LST), standardized within each city-year. Landsat 8/9 Collection 2 Level 2 · WorldPop (constrained model) · GHSL Urban Centre Database R2024A. Not a measure of absolute temperature — comparisons are only valid within a single city.
